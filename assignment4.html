A4: Subtractive Manufacturing

By Priyana Patel



I chose the 3D printing option for this week’s assignment. I created my box model in Rhino, separated and adjusted each panel of the box, sliced in Cura, printed using my Ender 3 Pro, and then connected the pieces to create my final box.


Modeling the Box

I decided to use Finger-Joints to connect my box, following a tutorial .stl files provided by the Jacobs Institute for Design Innovation at UC Berkeley (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XG8oABefLGU&t=332s). I first created five 4-inch by 4inch (40 mm by 40 mm) panels using the Box function in Rhino. Each board was initially 0.125 inches thick. I connected each panel to create a box, leaving 0.125 inches on each side. I filled these gaps with eight 0.5-inch by 0.125-inch boxes stacked on top of each other. These represent the finger-joints that will connect the box together. I had to scale certain boxes on the edge of each panel using the Scale1D function, ensuring the length matched each corner of the box.
After this, I used the BooleanUnion function to join the boxes and alternate joints. After connecting each panel with the joints, I hid the objects to make it easier to see which ones I had already connected. Once I joined all the sides, I rotated each panel and used Make2D to flatten the boards. I opened a new Rhino file and extruded the three sides back to 0.125 inches (one bottom and two adjoining sides) to see whether my pieces would fit.

Refinements

My first print made me realize that my panels were way too thin. I also printed the panels upright, which needed to be more sturdy and made the filament uneven. For my next print, I doubled the thickness to 0.250 inches, however, I forgot to offset the joints. Because of this, my panels were thick enough but did not fit together. I adjusted the joints by 0.250 inches for my third and final print, ensuring a tight fit. After a successful three-side test, I printed the other two panels and added a flat top (with no finger joints). With more time, I would add joints to all sides of the panels to ensure the top won’t come off.
